CAR BATTERY VS. ALTERNATOR: HOW TO TELL WHICH ONE FAILED

Your car won't start -- or worse, it died while you were driving. Is it the battery or the alternator? Here is how to figure it out in under five minutes.

The Battery Guys Team

April 27, 2026

THE FASTEST WAY TO TELL: JUMP START THE CAR

If your car will not start, here is the single best diagnostic you can do before calling anyone. Jump start the car -- use jumper cables from another vehicle or a portable jump pack -- and watch what happens next.

CAR RUNS FINE AFTER JUMP

The battery was the problem. It could not hold enough charge to start the engine, but once running, the alternator took over and kept everything powered. You need a new battery.

CAR DIES AGAIN WITHIN MINUTES

The alternator is the problem. The jump got the engine running, but without a working alternator to supply power and recharge the battery, the car stalled again. You likely need an alternator repair and possibly a new battery too.

This single test gives you the right answer about 90% of the time. But symptoms can overlap, so let us break down each failure in detail.

BATTERY FAILURE SYMPTOMS

A dead or dying battery is the most common reason a car will not start. Batteries have a finite lifespan -- typically three to five years in South Florida's heat, which accelerates chemical degradation inside the cells. Here is what a failing battery looks and sounds like.

  • Slow or sluggish cranking. You turn the key and the engine turns over slowly, almost groaning. This is the battery struggling to deliver enough amperage to the starter motor.
  • Clicking sound, no start. A rapid clicking when you turn the key means the battery has some charge but not enough to engage the starter. A single click usually points to the starter solenoid, but low voltage from a weak battery can cause this too.
  • Nothing at all. You turn the key and get silence -- no dash lights, no dome light, nothing. The battery is completely dead.
  • Battery is more than 3-4 years old. In Florida's heat, batteries age faster than the national average. If your battery is older than four years and giving you trouble, replacement is almost always the right call.
  • Swollen or corroded battery case. Visible bulging on the sides of the battery or heavy white/blue corrosion on the terminals are signs that the battery is failing internally.

The key pattern with a battery failure: the problem happens when you try to start the car. Once the engine is running, a bad battery usually does not cause issues because the alternator takes over.

ALTERNATOR FAILURE SYMPTOMS

The alternator is a generator bolted to your engine. It converts mechanical energy from the serpentine belt into electrical energy that powers your car's systems and recharges the battery while the engine runs. When it fails, the battery becomes the only power source -- and it will drain fast. Here is what alternator failure looks like.

  • Car dies while driving. This is the biggest red flag for alternator failure. If your car stalls or loses electrical power while the engine is running, the alternator is almost certainly the culprit. A bad battery alone will not cause this.
  • Dimming or flickering headlights. If your headlights pulse brighter and dimmer, especially at idle, the alternator is struggling to maintain consistent voltage output.
  • Dashboard warning light. Most vehicles have a battery-shaped icon or "ALT" / "GEN" light on the dash. If this light comes on while the engine is running, the charging system has a problem.
  • Burning rubber or electrical smell. A failing alternator can overheat or cause belt slippage, producing a hot-rubber or burnt-wire smell under the hood.
  • Whining or grinding noise from the engine bay. Worn bearings inside the alternator produce a distinctive high-pitched whine that changes with engine RPM. A grinding noise means the bearings are nearly seized.
  • Electrical accessories acting up. Power windows moving slowly, radio cutting out, or the AC blower weakening while driving -- all signs the alternator cannot supply enough current.

The key pattern with alternator failure: the problem shows up while the car is running. Systems dim, flicker, or shut down because the alternator is not generating enough power to keep up with demand.

HOW TO TEST WITH A MULTIMETER

If you have a basic digital multimeter (around $15 at any auto parts store), you can confirm your diagnosis in two minutes. Set the meter to DC volts (the V with a straight line, not the wavy line).

TEST 1: BATTERY VOLTAGE (ENGINE OFF)

Turn the car off. Connect the red multimeter lead to the positive (+) battery terminal and the black lead to the negative (-) terminal. Read the voltage.

12.4 - 12.7V

Battery is healthy

12.0 - 12.3V

Battery is low / partially discharged

Below 12.0V

Battery is dead or failing

TEST 2: CHARGING VOLTAGE (ENGINE RUNNING)

Start the engine (jump it if needed). With the engine running at idle, read the voltage across the battery terminals again.

13.7 - 14.7V

Alternator is charging properly

12.8 - 13.6V

Alternator is weak / undercharging

Below 12.8V

Alternator is not charging

If the engine-off reading is below 12.4V but the running voltage is in the 13.7-14.7V range, the alternator is fine -- your battery just could not hold a charge and needs replacement. If the running voltage stays below 13V, the alternator is the problem.

DIAGNOSTIC FLOWCHART

Here is the decision tree a mechanic walks through mentally. Follow it from the top.

1

Can you jump start the car?

If no: check battery connections, cables, and the starter motor. Could be a mechanical issue, not just electrical.

2

After the jump, does the car stay running?

If yes: proceed to step 3. If no (dies within 30 seconds to a few minutes): the alternator is failing.

3

Does the car start normally the next day?

If yes: you may have left a light on or the battery had a one-time deep discharge. Monitor it. If no (dead again the next morning): the battery cannot hold a charge. Replace it.

4

You replaced the battery but it keeps dying?

The alternator is not recharging it, or you have a parasitic electrical draw (something draining the battery when the car is off). Both need professional diagnosis.

CAN A BAD ALTERNATOR KILL A GOOD BATTERY?

Yes -- and it happens all the time. Here is the mechanics of why.

When your alternator fails, your car does not immediately stop. It keeps running on battery power alone. The battery was designed to provide a short burst of high current to start the engine and then immediately get recharged by the alternator. It was never meant to power the entire vehicle for an extended period.

Running on battery alone deeply discharges the cells. Lead-acid batteries (the standard type in most cars) suffer permanent damage when they are discharged below about 50% of their capacity. The lead sulfate crystals that form on the plates during discharge harden and become irreversible -- a process called sulfation. Once this happens, the battery loses capacity permanently.

What this means for your wallet

If you drive around with a bad alternator for even a few days, you will likely need to replace both the alternator and the battery. Catching the alternator problem early can save you the cost of a new battery. If your dashboard charging light comes on, do not ignore it.

A bad alternator can also overcharge a battery if the voltage regulator fails in the "on" position, pushing voltage above 15V. This causes the battery to overheat, boil its electrolyte, and swell. Both undercharging and overcharging will destroy a good battery.

WHAT THE BATTERY GUYS CHECK WHEN WE COME OUT

When you call us for a mobile battery replacement, we do not just slap in a new battery and leave. We test the entire charging system on the spot to make sure the real problem gets solved. Here is what we check.

Battery Load Test

We measure resting voltage and put the battery under a simulated starting load to see if it can deliver enough cold cranking amps. A battery can read 12.6V and still fail under load.

Alternator Output Test

We check the charging voltage at the battery terminals with the engine running. We also test under electrical load (headlights on, AC on) to see if the alternator can keep up with real-world demand.

Terminal and Cable Inspection

Corroded or loose terminals can mimic a dead battery. We clean the connections and check cable integrity before making any diagnosis.

Honest Recommendation

If we find the alternator is the problem, we will tell you. We are not going to sell you a battery you do not need. We test both so you get the right fix the first time.
