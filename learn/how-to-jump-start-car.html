HOW TO JUMP START A CAR: STEP-BY-STEP GUIDE

A dead battery can strand you in a parking lot, your own driveway, or worse -- the side of a busy highway. Knowing how to jump start a car is one of the most practical skills any driver can have. With a set of jumper cables and a second vehicle, you can get back on the road in about 15 minutes. This guide walks you through every step, explains the safety precautions that matter most, and tells you when a jump start will not fix the problem.

WHAT YOU WILL NEED

  • A set of jumper cables -- at least 12 feet long, 6-gauge or heavier. Longer cables give you more positioning flexibility.
  • A second vehicle with a fully charged, working battery.
  • Safety glasses and work gloves (recommended). Batteries contain sulfuric acid and can produce hydrogen gas.

JUMP START CAR STEPS: THE COMPLETE PROCESS

  1. 1

    Position the cars

    Park the working vehicle nose-to-nose or side-by-side with the dead car so the batteries are close together. Leave about 18 inches between the vehicles -- close enough for the cables to reach, but the cars should not be touching. Both vehicles should be on a flat, stable surface away from traffic if possible.

  2. 2

    Turn everything off

    Shut off both engines, headlights, radios, air conditioning, and every other electrical accessory. Put both vehicles in Park (automatic) or Neutral (manual) and engage the parking brakes. Remove the keys from the ignition of both cars.

  3. 3

    Identify the positive (+) and negative (-) terminals

    Open both hoods and locate the batteries. Each battery has two terminals: the positive (+) terminal, usually marked with a plus sign and covered by a red cap or housing, and the negative (-) terminal, marked with a minus sign and often covered by a black cap. If the terminals are corroded, brush off the buildup with a dry cloth before attaching cables.

  4. 4

    Connect the red cable to the dead battery's positive (+) terminal

    Pick up the red (positive) jumper cable. Clamp one end firmly onto the positive (+) terminal of the dead battery. Make sure the clamp has a solid bite on bare metal -- not just the plastic cap or corroded buildup.

  5. 5

    Connect the other red cable end to the good battery's positive (+) terminal

    Walk the other end of the same red cable to the working vehicle and clamp it to the positive (+) terminal of the good battery. You now have a positive-to-positive connection between the two batteries.

  6. 6

    Connect the black cable to the good battery's negative (-) terminal

    Pick up the black (negative) jumper cable. Clamp one end onto the negative (-) terminal of the good battery on the working vehicle.

  7. 7

    Ground the other black cable on the dead car -- NOT the battery terminal

    This is the most important safety step. Clamp the remaining end of the black cable to an unpainted metal surface on the engine block or frame of the dead car -- a bolt, bracket, or strut tower works well. Keep this clamp at least 12 inches from the battery.

    Why not the negative terminal?

    A dead or weak battery can release hydrogen gas -- a highly flammable byproduct of the chemical reaction inside the battery. When you connect the final cable, a small spark is normal. If that spark happens right next to the battery where hydrogen gas has accumulated, it can ignite the gas and cause the battery to explode. By grounding the cable on a metal surface away from the battery, any spark occurs far from the danger zone.

  8. 8

    Start the good car and wait 2-3 minutes

    Start the engine of the working vehicle and let it idle. Some people gently rev to around 1,500 RPM to push more charge through the cables. Wait at least 2 to 3 minutes so the dead battery can absorb enough charge to turn the starter motor.

  9. 9

    Try starting the dead car

    Turn the key or press the start button on the dead vehicle. If the engine cranks slowly, wait another 2 to 3 minutes and try again. If the car starts, let it idle -- do not turn it off. If it does not start after 3 or 4 attempts, the battery may be too far gone for a jump start, or there is another problem at play.

  10. 10

    Remove cables in reverse order

    Once the dead car is running, disconnect the cables in the exact reverse order they were connected:

    1. Black cable from the grounding point on the (formerly) dead car
    2. Black cable from the good battery's negative terminal
    3. Red cable from the good battery's positive terminal
    4. Red cable from the dead battery's positive terminal

    Do not let any clamp ends touch each other or touch both terminals of a battery simultaneously.

  11. 11

    Drive for at least 20-30 minutes to recharge

    Your alternator needs sustained driving time to push charge back into the battery. Take a 20- to 30-minute drive at highway speeds if you can. Avoid short trips or sitting at idle -- neither will fully recharge the battery. If the car dies again shortly after the jump, the battery likely needs to be replaced.

SAFETY WARNINGS

Jump starting a car involves live electrical connections and battery chemicals. Follow these precautions every single time.

  • Never let the clamp ends touch each other while any cable end is connected to a battery. This creates a short circuit that can spark, damage electronics, or cause injury.
  • Never jump a cracked, leaking, or visibly damaged battery. If you see cracks in the case, acid residue, or a swollen/bulging battery body, do not attempt a jump. The battery could rupture or explode.
  • Wear eye protection. Battery acid and sparks are both real hazards. A pair of basic safety glasses costs a few dollars and prevents a trip to the ER.
  • Do not smoke or use open flames near a battery. Hydrogen gas is colorless and odorless -- you will not know it is there until a spark ignites it.
  • Check your owner's manual first. Some newer vehicles (especially hybrids and EVs) have specific jump start procedures or designated jump points under the hood that differ from the standard setup.

WHEN A JUMP START WILL NOT WORK

A jump start is a temporary solution, not a cure. There are situations where no amount of cable time will bring your battery back:

  • The battery is more than 4-5 years old. Most car batteries last 3 to 5 years. If yours is past that window and keeps dying, it has reached the end of its useful life and needs replacement.
  • The battery dies repeatedly. If you successfully jump start the car but it dies again within a day or two, the battery can no longer hold a charge. A jump buys you one trip -- to the shop or to a mobile battery service.
  • Electrical systems or the starter motor are faulty. If you hear clicking but nothing else, the starter motor may be failing. If you see dim headlights that do not brighten after a jump, the alternator may not be charging the battery properly.
  • The battery is physically damaged. Cracks, leaks, swelling, or a strong sulfur (rotten egg) smell all mean the battery is compromised. Do not attempt a jump -- have it replaced immediately.
